Yin and Yang

Yin and yang are two primal, opposing, but complementary forces that give rise to all manifestations in the universe. They describe how seemingly opposite or contrary forces are interconnected and interdependent in the natural world, and how they give rise to each other in turn.  Yin is associated with qualities like darkness, femininity, coldness, and passivity, while yang represents brightness, masculinity, warmth, and activity. Yin and yang are fundamental concepts in Chinese philosophy.  They are regarded as the two complementary and interconnected aspects of everything in the universe. Odd numbers are considered yang and even numbers are yin. Yin and Yang also form the basis of the Five Elements theory.

Bees and Flowers

The relationship between bees and flowers exemplifies the Yin-Yang complementarity in Taoist and ancient Chinese understandings. It provides a genuine gateway to the subject. In Taiji, everything exists in a dynamic balance of yin (the passive force) and yang (the active force), and there is interconnection. The interaction between yin and yang is not just about achieving balance, however, but also includes the dynamic and constant transformation in all things.  At any point, yin and yang might be unbalanced or in conflict then head towards resolution. All things are in a state of change.

  • Interdependence: Bees and flowers are interdependent for their mutual survival and propagation. Bees rely on flowers for their source of nectar and pollen, while flowers rely on bees for pollination, ensuring their reproduction and continuation of their species.
  • Yin and Yang: In this context, the bee can be seen as representing the active, yang principle, while the flower embodies the passive, yin principle. The bee actively gathers nectar and pollinates flowers, exhibiting the dynamic, assertive qualities associated with yang. In contrast, the flower remains rooted, receiving the bee's actions, exemplifying the receptive, yielding nature of yin.
  • Harmonious Balance: Despite their opposing differences in form, bees and flowers exist in a harmonious balance, fulfilling their respective roles. The bee's yang energy is complemented by the flower's yin energy, creating a balanced and mutually beneficial relationship.
  • Cyclic Transformation: In the cycle of life, the bee and flower relationship reflects the constant transformation between yin and yang. The bee's pollination activities lead to the creation of new flowers, which in turn provide sustenance for future generations of bees, forming an endless cycle of interdependence and transformation.

This is a situation when seemingly opposing forces are interconnected, interdependent, and in constant flux, achieving a state of harmony and balance within the natural order.

The Pollen

As we will see by examining pollen, the Yin is present in Yang and Yang is in present Yin.  Which aspect of Yin and Yang appies will change depending on which angle and level is used to discuss the situation.

  • Pollen relative to flowers: We said that the flower is Yin relative to the bee, Yang.  Within the flower itself however, from a Taiji viewpoint, pollen represents the yang (active, creative) aspect of the flower's reproductive cycle. The flower, specifically the pistil (the female part of the flower), represents the yin aspect.

Pollen is a fine to coarse powdery substance comprising pollen grains, which are male microgametophytes of seed plants, which produce male gametes (sperm cells). It is the essence of the flower's masculine energy, containing genetic material necessary for creating new life. When bees transfer pollen between flowers, they facilitate the union of yang energies (the pollen) and yin (the receptive, feminine flower, to the pistil), allowing for the creation of seeds and the continuation of the plant's life cycle.  

  • When bees or other pollinators move from flower to flower in search of nectar or pollen as food, they often carry pollen grains on their bodies. This can lead to the transfer of pollen from the male part of a flower (the stamen, specifically the anther) to the female part of a flower (the pistil, specifically the stigma) of a different flower of the same species. This transfer is known as pollination.
  • The flower will have to get pollen from another flower.  Its own pollen should be made use of by a different flower.

  • Pollen to bees: For bees, pollen serves multiple functions that reflect Taiji’s mutual benefit and interdependence:
  • Nourishment (yin aspect): Pollen provides essential proteins, vitamins, and minerals that bees need for their own health and the development of young bees in the hive. This nurturing role represents the yin quality of pollen for bees.  So whereas the bees transport the pollen among flowers, they also use it themselves.
  • Energy source (yang aspect): Pollen, along with nectar, fuels the bees' activities, providing the energy they need for flight, foraging, and hive maintenance. This energizing function embodies the yang quality of pollen for bees.
  • Balance and harmony: The collection and use of pollen by bees exemplify the Taiji principle of balance. Bees take what they need from flowers while simultaneously providing a crucial service (pollination) to the plants. This mutually beneficial relationship demonstrates the harmonious interplay of seemingly opposite forces in nature.
  • Transformation: In Taiji philosophy, change and transformation are constant. Bees transform pollen into "bee bread" by mixing it with nectar and enzymes, creating a more easily digestible and storable food source. This transformation process reflects the dynamic nature of qi (life energy) as understood in Taiji.

From a Taiji perspective, pollen embodies the principle of dual purpose and interconnectedness. It serves as both a means of reproduction for flowers and a source of sustenance for bees, while also facilitating a larger cycle of ecological balance and mutual benefit. This exemplifies the Taiji concept of harmony through the interaction of complementary forces, where each element plays multiple roles in maintaining the overall balance of nature.

Once pollen grains land on the stigma (part of the pistil), they are nurtured and allowed to germinate, growing a pollen tube towards the ovule—a process that exemplifies the yin qualities of receptivity and nurturing.  This is called fertilization

Union of Yin and Yang: The transfer of pollen to the flower and the subsequent fertilization process can be seen as the union of yin and yang energies.

Seed Formation: The fertilized ovule develops into a seed, which contains the embryo (the young plant) and a food reserve (endosperm or cotyledons, depending on the plant type). The seed is a dormant structure that can remain inactive for various periods until conditions are right for germination.

Germination and New Plant Growth: When a seed is exposed to the right conditions (such as adequate water, oxygen, and sometimes light), it can germinate. Germination involves the embryo inside the seed beginning to grow, developing a root (radicle) that anchors into the soil and a shoot that grows towards the light. This process results in the establishment of a new plant, which, if it survives to maturity, can itself produce flowers, undergo pollination and fertilization, and produce seeds, thus continuing the life cycle.

The Nectar

In Taiji, nectar represents a balance of yin and yang energies within the flower

  • Nectar relative to the flower:
  • Yin aspect: Nectar is a nurturing, attractive force. It's the flower's way of inviting and sustaining pollinators, embodying the receptive, feminine principle of yin.
  • Yang aspect: Nectar production requires energy and resources from the plant, representing an active, outward-directed effort to ensure reproduction. This aligns with the yang principle of active creation.
  • Transformation: In Taiji, Yang endows and Yin transforms. The flower transforms (Yin) its resources (Yang) into nectar, exemplifying the Taiji concept of constant change and the flow of qi (life energy).

  • Nectar to bees (for the hive or the bee itself):

For bees, nectar serves multiple functions that manifest Taiji principles:

  • Energy source (yang aspect): Nectar provides carbohydrates, serving as the primary energy source for bees. This fuels their active, yang-oriented tasks such as flying, foraging, and maintaining the hive.
  •  Honey production (yin aspect): Bees transform nectar into honey through a process of regurgitation and evaporation. This stored food represents the yin principle of preservation and nurturing, ensuring the hive's survival during times of scarcity.
  • Balance and harmony: The relationship between bees and nectar demonstrates the Taiji concept of mutual benefit. Bees receive sustenance while flowers receive pollination services, illustrating the harmonious interplay of seemingly opposite forces in nature.
  • Cycle of transformation: The process of collecting nectar, converting it to honey, and using it for energy exemplifies the Taiji principle of cyclic transformation. Energy flows from flower to bee to hive and back to the environment in a continuous cycle.
  • Unity of opposites: Nectar represents both a gift from the flower and a reward for the bee's pollination service. This dual nature reflects the Taiji concept that apparent opposites are actually complementary aspects of a greater whole.
  • Adaptability: Different flowers produce different types and amounts of nectar, and bees must adapt their foraging strategies accordingly. This reflects the Taiji principle of flexibility and responsiveness to change.

Thus at different levels and aspects of the relationship between bees and flowers manifest yin and yang promptly.

The Five Elements

In traditional Chinese culture, the Five Elements are known as the fundamental components of the universe.  They comprise every phenomenon in the universe, including all matter.  They are Water, Wood, Fire, Earth, and Metal.  Each one is composed of the more primal forces of yin and yang. A specific element's unique proportion of yin and yang gives rise to its distinctive properties and behaviors.  The relationships occurring between these elements can also be described in terms of yin and yang. Thus, this ancient Chinese scientific theory is complete in its means of description.  

Contrary to modern interpretations of the Five Elements, they are in fact matter, they are not symbolic at any point.  It is that the ancient understanding of matter was altogether different and encompassed more dimensions of experience, contained more that was known to be alive, much of which we currently regard as conceptual in a modern scientific frame of thinking.  To us, a season would not be composed of matter but is just a concept.  In ancient times, everything is alive and part of the larger whole with the smaller being seen in the larger and vice versa.  Since a season has been divinely arranged as part of the natural order of things progressing, it actually is composed of matter, one of the Five Elements.  Reality expands into the material therefore, making this theory much more powerful than modern science.

Understanding the Five elements requires a shift in mindset, as initially it does not make sense to the modern mind that parts of the world would be composed of Wood when this is a material found in plants.  Additionally, it takes more than the conventional accounts and diagrams a person can find online and contemporary books and knowledge to grasp because in fact much has been lost to the empirical industry. To be able to realize what it is about you have to go beyond what is taught in Traditional Chinese Medicine in modern times because in actuality they have only partially correct understandings and are not practitioners of a cultivation path.

This Five Elements theory is not to be equated with something like a “Five Atomic Elements” and then the names of things found on our planet or experienced by people placed underneath those five categories.   That is our initial inclination, but it isn’t the right understanding. Wood as an element embodies a spectrum of general characteristics, states, relationships, and attributes that are not part of modern scientific descriptions when discussing particles: growth, decline, undermining, supporting, springing forth, moving out of dormancy into activity, etc. aren’t the terms used.   These are states and qualities that are not part of our modern scientific investigation and discourse.  One could say that we are cut off from them because they sound so irrelevant to modern thinking.  Coinciding with that, these attributes are reflecting the spiritual aspect embodied in the universe that the progressive emphasis on the mundane throughout history has now occluded for us.

An atomic element is a modern scientific theory investigating matter while speaking in terms of laws of phenomenal, behavioral properties and states of matter.  What will the chemical do, how does it interact, and under what conditions does it do a thing or refuse to do a thing?  That basically describes the whole of modern science in its objectives to understand the world.  The Five Elements emphasizes what we know in everyday life to be true: everything in the universe is in a process of changing, rising and falling.  The two approaches are fundamentally different.

The Standard Circle Diagram

Typically the Five Elements are introduced by presenting them in the form of a circular diagram that serves to illustrate how they affect and transition into one another.  It presents the direction of effect in two cycles, called generating and controlling.  In the generating cycle, a given element will produce or support the element that sits next to it moving in the clockwise direction.  In the controlling cycle, an element controls, undermines, or insults the element that sits two steps over moving in the clockwise direction, indicated by the arrows that are internal to the circle.

In the above arrangement, the diagram only conveys the fact that there are two cycles, with one supporting and one insulting. This is far from enough for the beginner. A person will inevitably ask how Metal produces Water in the same way that Wood obviously produces Fire.  It does not, and there should be clarification as to why that is the case, because otherwise the theory sounds nebulous.

  • When Wood generates the Fire element, and the Fire generates Earth, these two instances manifest the yang side of the generative cycle.  Wood acts directly as the medium for Fire to burn and when Fire burns it directly produces ash, which makes more Earth.  So in both cases the elements are providing overt support or generation.  

  • Earth sits somewhere in between this and something more passive: in its support for the Metal element, it both bears (yin) and refines (yang) Metal in the ground.  

  • When we get to the yin aspects of the support/generative cycle, they are playing an indirect role in the production of the next element: Metal is not part of the production of Water but provides conditions for it to form, such as condensation.  This is the yin aspect side of the generative cycle; these two elements are not the actual media that produces the next element.  Water produces Wood in the same way. Water nourishes plants, a yin activity, but this does not directly comprise the wood material, the substantial part of the plant.

When the theory is so foreign and inscrutable when perceived with our modern minds, the conventional cycles diagram needs to be expanded or provided notes to make the theory understandable.  Especially when this vague diagram is paired with a table or list of phenomena associated with each element, the diagram can give a shallow impression of the theory and relegate it to a collection of historical information. The person’s understanding becomes flat, inaccurate, and oriented towards rote memorization of categories instead of an understanding. This is because the Five Elements are not merely a set of classifications for phenomena but a deep, interwoven system of principles that apply to things large and small in the universe.  The associations themselves should make the Five Elements clearer and how they embody yin and yang.  To receive that impression of the theory requires a different entry point than what one finds in most books today.

Converting the Circle Diagram Into a Wave

As the path of a circle is followed, it is unending. Matching this, the form of a circle can be represented as a continuous wave, as taught in trigonometry, to embody more aspects of the Five Elements in a diagram and reformulate its presentation.  We plot the same associations along the corresponding wave.  First, the proportion of yin and yang at each point and in each element is made more obvious when they are positioned along a wave.  Furthermore, we generally perceive time linearly today and so this arrangement makes it easier to follow the progression of the Five Elements over time, whether it is the seasons of the year or the hours of the day. Since our circle diagram is divided into five portions, this sine wave is as well.  On our graph, the y-axis plots Yang in the positive (up) direction and Yin in the negative (down) direction.  The x-axis represents the progression of time if the elements are viewed in their relation to time, but this arrangement also functions as a chart.

For the benefit of the newcomer, this wave presentation expresses the interconnection, associations, and phases of the Five Elements in such a way that there is more integrated information.

Controlling Relationships, The Controlling (Kè) Cycle

The other process is the controlling (kè) one.  An element has a controlling effect over an element that is one steps past the one that it generates.  So, whereas the qualities of Winter (Water) produce or generates Spring (Wood), when talking about what Water controls it is Fire (Summer), which is one step past Spring. Water controls Fire; the set of qualities found in Winter control the qualities found in Summer.

In each instance there is a range of how much control the stronger element might exhibit over the subordinate one.  Water might be cooling off the heat and thereby controlling it, or it might be extinguishing the fire, destroying it.  Metal might be either pruning or felling a tree. As such, Ke is variously translated as controlling, insulting, or destroying.  It is acceptable to call kè the Controlling Cycle, Insulting Cycle, or Destroying Cycle.  An element might be doing one of these three to its receiver.

Understanding this kè process can take place by examining the relationship between the controlling element and the controlled as one of strength of firmness or ability to break up the receiving element.

The ke (controlling) cycle in the Wuxing (Five Elements) system can be understood in terms of an increasing gradient of firmness that repeats in which a firmer element generally controls, insults, or in the more intense case, destroys a less firm element.

We will discuss the cycle as a progression of firmness flows from the most firm element down. Metal (most firm) to Wood, Earth, Water, and finally to Fire (least firm). The Wu Xing, or Five Elements theory in Chinese philosophy, associates specific flavors with each element.   1. Wood (木 mù): Sour
  2. Fire (火 huǒ): Bitter
  3. Earth (土 tǔ): Sweet
  4. Metal (金 jīn): Spicy/Pungent
  5. Water (水 shuǐ): Salty

How they go together:

  1. Generating (生 shēng) cycle:
  • Wood generates Fire: Sour enhances Bitter
  • Fire generates Earth: Bitter supports Sweet
  • Earth generates Metal: Sweet complements Spicy
  • Metal generates Water: Spicy balances Salty
  • Water generates Wood: Salty enriches Sour
  1. Controlling (克 kè) cycle:
  • Wood controls Earth: Sour moderates Sweet
  • Earth controls Water: Sweet tempers Salty
  • Water controls Fire: Salty reduces Bitter
  • Fire controls Metal: Bitter counteracts Spicy
  • Metal controls Wood: Spicy cuts through Sour
  1. Balancing principles:
  • Each flavor can be used to balance excesses of others
  • Combinations should aim for harmony rather than dominance
  • Seasonal eating often emphasizes flavors associated with the current element
  1. Culinary applications:
  • Sour and Sweet: Common in many cuisines (e.g., sweet and sour sauce)
  • Salty and Sweet: Enhances overall flavor profile (e.g., salted caramel)

  • Bitter and Sweet: Complementary in beverages (e.g., coffee with sugar)
  • Salty and Bitter: Can balance rich foods (e.g., dark chocolate with sea salt)

The Book of Changes

The Book of Changes, also known as the I Ching or Yi Jing, is an ancient Chinese divination text and the oldest of the Chinese classics. It is a profound and complex work that has been used for over three millennia for its wisdom, guidance, and philosophical insights. The I Ching consists of 64 hexagrams, each composed of six lines that are either broken (yin) or unbroken (yang). These hexagrams are used as a form of divination to provide answers to questions or guidance in decision-making.  The I Ching is not only a divination manual but also a profound philosophical work that reflects the ancient Chinese understanding of the universe and the principles of change. It embodies the concepts of yin and yang, the Tao, and the interconnectedness of all things. The book teaches that change is the only constant in the universe and that understanding the patterns of change can lead to harmony and balance in one's life.

The Trigrams

The following trigrams are the building blocks of the I Ching's 64 hexagrams, each of which is formed by combining two trigrams. The combination of trigrams in a hexagram creates a more complex symbol that conveys a specific message or situation, often offering guidance or insight into the querent's life or the question at hand

In the I Ching, each trigram is composed of three lines, which can be either broken (yin) or unbroken (yang). The arrangement of these yin and yang lines gives each trigram its specific meaning and character.

Each trigram represents a fundamental concept or natural element.

Reading a Trigram

To read a trigram, you start from the bottom line and move up to the top line. This is because the I Ching is based on the idea of change and development, and things often start from a foundation and grow or evolve.

Here's how to interpret a trigram from the bottom to the top:

  1. Bottom Line: This line represents the beginning or the foundation of the situation. It indicates the initial conditions or the starting point of the energy or action described by the trigram.

  1. Middle Line: Moving up to the middle line, this position represents the development or the process of the situation. It shows how the initial conditions are evolving or how the energy is being channeled or transformed.

  1. Top Line: Finally, the top line represents the outcome or the culmination of the situation. It indicates the result of the process described by the trigram or the final expression of the energy.

The middle and top lines are both broken (yin), suggesting the expansion and release of this energy as it moves upward, symbolizing the sudden and forceful nature of thunder.

Reading a trigram from the bottom to the top allows you to understand the dynamic flow of energy or the development of a situation over time, from its inception to its conclusion. This method of interpretation is consistent with the I Ching's focus on change and the unfolding of events.

Trigrams and Hexagrams

When interpreting trigrams and hexagrams in the I Ching, there are several additional aspects to consider.

  • Yin and Yang Balance: Each line in a trigram or hexagram is either yin (broken) or yang (unbroken). The balance of yin and yang lines within the figure can provide insight into the balance or imbalance of forces at play in the situation being described.

  • Interaction of Trigrams: In a hexagram, which is composed of two trigrams (one on top of the other), the interaction between the upper and lower trigrams is crucial. The combination of the two trigrams creates a more complex symbol that conveys a specific message or situation. The relationship between the trigrams can be one of harmony, conflict, or other dynamics, depending on their nature.

  • Changing Lines: When consulting the I Ching, you may encounter "changing lines," which are indicated by a different marking (often an x or a circle) on a yin or yang line. A changing line suggests that the situation is in flux and may change. The interpretation of a hexagram with changing lines involves considering both the original hexagram and the new hexagram that would result if the changing line(s) were transformed (yin to yang or yang to yin).

  • Numerical Significance: Each line in a hexagram is also associated with a number, starting from the bottom (sixth line) to the top (first line). These numbers can be used in various calculations or interpretations, such as in the use of the King Wen sequence, which assigns a specific order and number to each hexagram.

  • Textual Commentary: The I Ching includes rich textual commentary for each hexagram, including the Judgment, Image, and Line Statements. These texts provide further insight into the meaning of the hexagram and its lines, offering guidance and wisdom related to the situation at hand.

  • Contextual Interpretation: The meaning of a trigram or hexagram should be interpreted in the context of the question or situation for which the I Ching was consulted. The same hexagram can have different meanings depending on the context in which it is received.

  • Symbolic Representation: Trigrams and hexagrams are symbolic representations of complex ideas and situations. They do not provide literal answers but rather offer symbolic guidance that requires reflection and interpretation to apply to one's life or the question at hand.

Historical Background

The origins of the I Ching are obscure, but it is believed to have been compiled over a long period, with the earliest strata dating back to the Western Zhou dynasty (1046–771 BCE). The text was traditionally attributed to the mythical Chinese emperor Fu Xi, who is said to have observed the patterns of the natural world and created the eight trigrams (combinations of three lines). Later, the hexagrams were developed by combining two trigrams, and the text was expanded and commented upon by various scholars, most notably Confucius and his disciples.

Structure and Use

The I Ching's 64 hexagrams are arranged in a specific order, known as the King Wen sequence, which is believed to reflect the natural flow of change and the cycles of life. Each hexagram is accompanied by a description, known as the "Judgment," and each line within the hexagram also has its own interpretation. The text also includes various commentaries, the most important of which are the "Ten Wings," which are attributed to Confucius and his followers.

To consult the I Ching, one traditionally uses either yarrow stalks or coins to randomly generate a hexagram. The process involves a series of random selections that result in the six lines of the hexagram, which can be either static or changing. Changing lines are those that are in the process of transforming from yin to yang or vice versa, and they indicate a situation that is in flux. The resulting hexagram, along with any changing lines, provides the basis for the reading, which offers insights, advice, or predictions based on the text's interpretations.

Philosophical Significance

The I Ching's influence extends beyond China, having been translated into numerous languages and inspiring thinkers, artists, and scholars around the world. Its teachings on the dynamics of change, the importance of balance, and the interplay of opposites have resonated with many, offering a timeless wisdom that continues to be relevant in the modern world.

The Book of Changes is a rich and multifaceted work that has played a significant role in Chinese culture and philosophy for centuries. Its enduring appeal lies in its ability to offer guidance and wisdom in the face of life's uncertainties, while also providing a framework for understanding the fundamental principles of the universe. Whether used for divination, philosophical reflection, or both, the I Ching remains a source of inspiration and insight for people around the globe.

Hetu and Luoshu

Hetu and Luoshu are two ancient diagrams in Chinese culture, often associated with cosmology, numerology, and the I Ching (Yijing). They are believed to contain profound meanings and have been influential in various aspects of Chinese thought, including philosophy, feng shui, and Taoism. Despite their significance, the exact origins of these diagrams are shrouded in mystery, and they are often surrounded by myth and legend.

Hetu

Hetu (河图), literally meaning "River Chart," is one of the two fundamental diagrams in Chinese metaphysics. According to legend, the Hetu was revealed on the back of a dragon-horse (qilin) that emerged from the Yellow River during the reign of the mythological Emperor Fu Xi (c. 2852 BCE - 2737 BCE). The Hetu is depicted as a chart with black and white dots arranged in a specific pattern. These dots are grouped in sets of one to nine and are believed to represent the fundamental principles of the universe, including the five elements (wood, fire, earth, metal, and water), yin and yang, and the cardinal directions.

Luoshu

Luoshu (洛书), or "Luo River Writing," is the other fundamental diagram. Legend has it that the Luoshu was presented on the back of a turtle from the Luo River during the reign of the legendary Emperor Yu the Great (c. 2205 BCE - 2198 BCE). The Luoshu is represented as a magic square, a grid of numbers where the sum of the numbers in each row, column, and diagonal is the same. The traditional Luoshu is a 3x3 grid with the numbers one through nine arranged in such a way that each line adds up to fifteen. This diagram is also deeply symbolic, representing the harmony and balance of the universe.

Hetu vs. Luoshu

While both the Hetu and Luoshu are revered for their cosmological and numerological significance, they are distinct in their symbolism and legend of origin:

Origin and Legend: The Hetu is associated with the Yellow River and Emperor Fu Xi, while the Luoshu is linked to the Luo River and Emperor Yu the Great.

Representation: The Hetu is depicted with dots representing numbers and elements, focusing on the five elements and their interactions. The Luoshu is represented as a magic square, emphasizing mathematical harmony and balance.

Symbolism: Both diagrams are rich in symbolism, but the Hetu is often seen as representing the natural order and the Luoshu as embodying the principles of governance and social order.

In Chinese metaphysics, the Hetu and Luoshu are considered complementary, representing different aspects of the universe and its underlying principles. They have been influential in various fields, including philosophy, divination, and the organization of space in accordance with feng shui principles
