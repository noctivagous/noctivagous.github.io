The Five Elements on a 2D Graph

[single sine wave]

The Five Elements are arranged as a wave on a 2D graph.  The y-axis plots Yang in the positive direction and Yin in the negative direction.  We can discuss the elements in terms of the seasons, and this brings out many qualities that are associated with each one.

WATER  (水, Shuǐ)

Water is plotted at the trough of the wave.  Corresponding to this in the physical world is any natural trough, a low spot where water settles during cold seasons. The Water element is associated with what is Winter in many regions.  Even if precipitation is not the greatest during wintertime, water nevertheless lingers more in this time, either through snow or moisture.  This time of year is generally cold, animals are dormant, and the daylight hours are at their shortest while the nighttime hours are at their longest; it is maximum Yin and minimum Yang. The energy is at its most inward and still, reflecting deep stillness and potential for renewal.  In the human life cycle, Winter (Water) represents old age, a period of life that has slowed down greatly, characterized by rest, reflection, and the accumulation of wisdom.

In terms of the time of day, Winter (Water) is associated with night, particularly midnight, when the world is at its darkest and most still. Emotionally, Winter (Water) is associated with fear which is always concealed by the person, also reflecting the stillness and introspection of the season. In traditional Chinese medicine, Winter (Water) corresponds to the kidneys and bladder, organs associated with storing and purifying. Natural phenomena for Winter (Water) include snow, ice, and dormancy, reflecting the stillness and inward energy of the season. The colors associated with Winter (Water) are black and dark blue, colors that reflect the depth and stillness of the season. In terms of direction, Winter (Water) is associated with the north, reflecting cold and stillness. The flavor associated with Winter (Water) is salty, reflecting the preserving and purifying qualities of the season. In terms of developmental stages in projects or work, Winter (Water) represents conception and planning, a time of inward reflection and preparation.

WOOD  (木, Mù)

Moving up from the trough towards the rising part of the wave, we encounter the Wood element. Wood is associated with what is Spring in many regions. This time of year is characterized by growth and expansion, as plants begin to sprout and temperatures warm. The energy in this phase is marked by rising Yang, with Yang increasing and Yin decreasing. This period signifies new beginnings and development, as life emerges from the dormancy of Winter.  In the human life cycle, Spring (Wood) corresponds to childhood, marked by rapid growth, learning, and development.

In terms of the time of day, Spring (Wood) corresponds to the morning, specifically dawn, when light begins to return and energy starts to rise. Emotionally, Spring (Wood) is linked to anger, an emotion that bursts out and can drive growth and change. In traditional Chinese medicine, Spring (Wood) is linked to the liver and gallbladder, organs that manage growth and detoxification. Natural phenomena for Spring (Wood) include blossoming, growth, and rain, symbolizing renewal and expansion. The color associated with Spring (Wood) is green, symbolizing growth and renewal. In terms of direction, Spring (Wood) corresponds to the east, symbolizing new beginnings and growth. The flavor associated with Spring (Wood) is sour, symbolizing growth and detoxification. In terms of developmental stages in projects or work, Spring (Wood) corresponds to initiation and start, marking the beginning of action and growth.

FIRE  (火, Huǒ)

At the peak of the wave, we find the Fire element. Fire is associated with what is Summer in many regions. This season is marked by full bloom, intense heat, and the longest daylight hours of the year. During this time, Yang is at its maximum, representing the peak of activity and energy, while Yin is at its minimum. This phase embodies the zenith of outward expression and vitality, with nature at its most vibrant and energetic.  In the human life cycle, Summer (Fire) symbolizes adolescence and young adulthood, a time of peak energy, passion, and activity.

In terms of the time of day, Summer (Fire) is linked to midday, when the sun is at its highest and energy peaks. Emotionally, Summer (Fire) corresponds to joy, which exudes from the surface of the person, the peak of emotional expression and energy. In traditional Chinese medicine, Summer (Fire) corresponds to the heart and small intestine, organs associated with circulation and digestion. Natural phenomena for Summer (Fire) include heat, sunshine, and full bloom, representing peak energy and activity. The color associated with Summer (Fire) is red, representing energy and passion. In terms of direction, Summer (Fire) is linked to the south, representing peak energy and heat. The flavor associated with Summer (Fire) is bitter, representing peak energy and intensity. In terms of developmental stages in projects or work, Summer (Fire) is linked to execution and peak activity, representing full engagement and energy.

EARTH (土, Tǔ)

Descending from the peak, we arrive at the Earth element. Earth is associated with what is often seen as Late Summer in many regions, a transitional period between Summer and Autumn. This time symbolizes stability and nurturing, with a balance between Yin and Yang. The energy during this period is in equilibrium, representing a harmonious state where neither Yin nor Yang dominates. It is a time of consolidation and preparation for the upcoming changes.  In the human life cycle, Late Summer (Earth) represents adulthood, characterized by stability, nurturing responsibilities, and maturity.

In terms of the time of day, Late Summer (Earth) is related to the afternoon, a time of stability and reflection. Emotionally, Late Summer (Earth) is associated with pensiveness, reflecting the nurturing and stable qualities of the season. In traditional Chinese medicine, Late Summer (Earth) is linked to the spleen and stomach, organs related to nourishment and digestion. Natural phenomena for Late Summer (Earth) include harvest, maturity, and abundance, reflecting stability and nourishment. The colors associated with Late Summer (Earth) are yellow and brown, colors that reflect stability and nourishment. In terms of direction, Late Summer (Earth) is associated with the center, symbolizing stability and balance. The flavor associated with Late Summer (Earth) is sweet, reflecting nourishment and stability. In terms of developmental stages in projects or work, Late Summer (Earth) corresponds to stabilization and review, a time of consolidation and reflection.

METAL  (金, Jīn)

Further down the wave, we reach the Metal element. Metal is associated with what is Autumn in many regions. This time of year is characterized by harvest and preparation for the colder months ahead, with cooling temperatures and shorter daylight hours. The energy in this season is marked by waning Yang, with Yang decreasing and Yin increasing. This phase signifies contraction and introspection, as the energy begins to move inward in preparation for the stillness and dormancy of Winter.  In the human life cycle, Autumn (Metal) is linked to middle age, a period of harvest, reflection, and preparation for the later stages of life.

In terms of the time of day, Autumn (Metal) corresponds to evening, particularly dusk, when the day begins to wane, and energies start to contract. Emotionally, Autumn (Metal) is linked to grief, a time of letting go and introspection. In traditional Chinese medicine, Autumn (Metal) corresponds to the lungs and large intestine, organs involved in respiration and elimination. Natural phenomena for Autumn (Metal) include falling leaves, wind, and dryness, symbolizing contraction and preparation for rest. The colors associated with Autumn (Metal) are white and silver, symbolizing clarity and reflection. In terms of direction, Autumn (Metal) corresponds to the west, representing reflection and contraction. The flavor associated with Autumn (Metal) is pungent or spicy, symbolizing clarity and movement. In terms of developmental stages in projects or work, Autumn (Metal) represents completion and reflection, marking the end of a cycle and preparation for renewal.

Each element, corresponding to its respective season, forms part of the dynamic and cyclical wave of energy, illustrating the balance and transformation of Yin and Yang throughout the year.

The above description describes the Five Elements in terms of progression of seasons.  The seasons lead into one another, each gradually transitions into the one that follows it which mirrors the Five Elements in how they transform and generate.  One element will lead to, produce, or generate the next in a cycle and the order of these seasons corresponds to that. This is the generative cycle (shēng) of the Five Elements.  The conventional four seasons are given the insertion of Late Summer before Autumn to make five.  The seasons make it easy to remember the order of the generating cycle.

Controlling Relationships, The Controlling (Kè) Cycle

The other process is the controlling (kè) one.  An element has a controlling effect over an element that is one steps past the one that it generates.  So, whereas the qualities of Winter (Water) produce or generates Spring (Wood), when talking about what Water controls it is Fire (Summer), which is one step past Spring. Water controls Fire; the set of qualities found in Winter control the qualities found in Summer.

In each instance there is a range of how much control the stronger element might exhibit over the subordinate one.  Water might be cooling off the heat and thereby controlling it, or it might be extinguishing the fire, destroying it.  Metal might be either pruning or felling a tree. As such, Ke is variously translated as controlling, insulting, or destroying.  It is acceptable to call kè the Controlling Cycle, Insulting Cycle, or Destroying Cycle.  An element might be doing one of these three to its receiver.

Understanding this kè process can take place by examining the relationship between the controlling element and the controlled as one of strength of firmness or ability to break up the receiving element.

The ke (controlling) cycle in the Wuxing (Five Elements) system can be understood in terms of an increasing gradient of firmness that repeats in which a firmer element generally controls, insults, or in the more intense case, destroys a less firm element.

We will discuss the cycle as a progression of firmness flows from the most firm element down. Metal (most firm) to Wood, Earth, Water, and finally to Fire (least firm). However, the cycle completes itself with a fascinating exception: Fire, the least firm element, controls Metal, the most firm. This unique relationship demonstrates the cyclical nature of the system and the transformative power of energy over structure. Each controlling relationship exhibits a range of interactions, from subtle influence to dramatic transformation.

Metal controlling Wood:

Metal, being firmer than Wood, can shape and limit it. A pruning shear can guide a plant's growth, demonstrating control. In the extreme, an axe can fell a tree, showing destruction. This range illustrates how precision can either direct or completely halt growth.

Metal's interaction with Wood is characterized by its sharpness and precision. This process’ relationship is often quick and definitive: chopping or slicing. Metal tools like axes, saws, or knives can easily cut through wood, shaping it decisively. In nature, this might be seen in how metal-rich soils can stunt plant growth. In human contexts, this could represent how clear, incisive thinking (Metal) can cut through and organize complex ideas or plans (Wood).

Wood controlling Earth:

Wood, firmer than Earth, can penetrate and restructure it. Plant roots can aerate soil, improving its quality, as a form of control. In the extreme, roots can break apart rocks and concrete over time, demonstrating destruction. This shows how growth, associated with Wood, can take place persistently and over time, which can either enhance or completely alter stable structures.

Wood's effect on Earth is typically a gradual process. Tree roots slowly grow through soil and rock, breaking them apart over time. This is a persistent, patient form of control. In less extreme cases, roots can improve soil structure by creating channels for air and water. This relationship might symbolize how steady personal growth can gradually change one's foundation or how innovative ideas can slowly transform established systems.

Earth controlling Water:

Earth, as a more solid element than Water, can contain and direct liquid Water. Riverbanks shape a river's flow, exemplifying control. In the extreme, a dam can completely stop a river, illustrating destruction. This range shows how stability can either guide or totally impede fluid movement.  There is constant surrounding or underlying of Water by Earth.

Water controlling Fire:

Water, while fluid, can temper and limit Fire. Steam can regulate heat in a system, demonstrating control. In the extreme, water can completely extinguish a fire, showing destruction. This illustrates how adaptability can either moderate or eliminate intense energy. There can be blanketing or dampening of Fire by Water.

Fire controlling Metal:

Fire, though least the firm of the Five Elements in terms of form, is the most active and can shape and transform rigid Metal. A controlled flame can temper metal, increasing its strength, as a form of control. In the extreme, intense heat can melt metal completely, demonstrating destruction. This range shows how transformative energy can either enhance or completely break down structure." Liquifying or increasing the malleability of Metal by Fire.

Metal has so much yin that it is rigid but not so much yin that it becomes inert.

The Five Elements Discussed in Terms of Yin and Yang

Each element is characterized by a unique balance of yin and yang forces, which gives rise to its distinctive properties and behaviors. Yin represents qualities such as coolness, contraction, and inward movement, while yang embodies heat, expansion, and outward motion. The interplay between these complementary forces within each element creates a dynamic system that explains various phenomena in nature, health, and human affairs. Here we discuss how the proportions of yin and yang manifest in the properties of each of the Five Elements:

  1. Fire: Maximum yang, minimum yin. Fire has the highest yang and lowest yin, resulting in its gaseous, active, and energetic nature. This manifests in several key properties:
  • Expansive energy: High yang leads to rapid expansion and spread
  • Heat: Maximum yang manifests as intense heat and light
  • Upward movement: Strong yang energy causes rising motion
  • Transformation: High yang enables rapid change and conversion of matter
  • Low density: Minimum yin results in a lack of substance, leading to gaseous form

Lack of yin means lack of structure, form, and stability to fire.  It likewise does not adapt to anything in front of it or follow a provided path but goes its own way and expands, actively.  By contrast, maximum yin is characterized by stillness.

  1. Earth: Balanced yin and yang. Earth represents stability and balance, with roughly equal proportions of yin and yang. This balance manifests in the following ways:
  • Stability: Equal yin and yang create a grounded, stable nature
  • Nourishment: Balance allows for nurturing qualities
  • Centrality: Acts as a neutral point between other elements
  • Receptivity: Yin aspect allows absorption and containment
  • Production: Yang aspect enables growth and generation
  1. Metal: High yin, low yang. Metal's high yin and low yang composition explains its firm nature through several mechanisms:
  • Condensation: The high yin energy represents a condensing, contracting force, leading to increased density and firmness
  • Structure: Yin energy is associated with form and structure, contributing to its rigid, crystalline structure
  • Coolness: Yin is related to coolness, manifesting as resistance to deformation and a stable atomic structure
  • Inward-directed energy: Low yang means less outward, expansive energy, resulting in atoms bonding tightly, creating a solid, firm substance

Yin energy contributes to form and structure, making Metal rigid but not so much yin is normally present that Metal becomes a liquid like Water.  Mercury would be said to have a higher proportion of yin to make it normally a liquid.

Transforming Metal to Liquid: To transform Metal from its solid state to a liquid state, an infusion of Yang energy is necessary. This is where the Fire element comes into play.

  1. Water: Maximum yin, minimum yang. Water has the highest yin and lowest yang, which manifests as fluidity, adaptability, and downward movement. Water will flow and fill any shape or container, representing the ultimate form of receptivity and flexibility. This is why liquid water is seen as the embodiment of maximum Yin.  Its properties include:
  • Fluidity: High yin energy allows for adaptable, flowing nature
  • Coolness: Maximum yin manifests as a cooling, calming influence
  • Downward movement: Strong yin energy causes sinking or descending motion
  • Shapelessness: High yin leads to lack of fixed form, taking shape of its container
  • Depth: Strong yin creates potential for depth and accumulation

Even though maximum Yin produces water, Yin energy as a whole is crucial for the formation and maintenance of structures. This duality can be understood through the different manifestations of Yin at various stages and in different contexts. While water represents Yin's fluidity and adaptability, Yin also provides the stability necessary for form and structure, as seen in the Metal element.

  1. Wood: Moderate yang, moderate yin. Wood has a balance leaning slightly towards yang, resulting in growth, flexibility, and upward expansion, but with more structure than Fire. This is evident in its characteristics:
  • Growth: Slightly higher yang promotes upward and outward growth
  • Flexibility: Balance of yin and yang allows for bendability without breaking
  • Vitality: Moderate yang provides life force and energy
  • Structure: Moderate yin gives form and substance to support growth
  • Cyclical nature: Balance enables seasonal changes and regeneration

Modern Science Versus The Five Elements

In the West, “reality” is considered a philosophical topic separate from investigation of the physical world.  In ancient China the Wu Xing (Five Elements) - Water, Wood, Fire, Earth, and Metal - were regarded as the fundamental components of what we call “reality.”  One could say they are more deep as elementary components; they extend beyond discrete, particulate units of the material, mechanistic reality we perceive in our modern age, which is what our atomic elements provide us conceptually.  If anything exists outside of these atomic elements or their subcomponents, we tend to believe it is our own human concepts projected onto the outside world.  If the opposite is permitted, that abstract ideas like the seasons, feelings, tastes, sounds, and colors are not byproducts of our own psychological experience, the Five Elements can be real and present in everything.  The world then has spiritual existence in addition to material presence.

In our modern times, we believe our world reality to be essentially molecularly-composed, all of it following physical laws of motion and interactions, whatever the latest physical theory. What we engage with culturally and personally in this essentially dispassionate world of motion and matter are the concepts we have projected onto it.  In our now deeply-ingrained conception, nothing exists more than the motion of matter, its reactions, and how it can be described— now that we have all matured as a species.  In other words, we believe we live, basically, in a mundane space of interacting molecules and whatever subatomic elements and patterns coincide with all of it, from sounds to light.  We have come to think of the matter out there as without spirit, merely conforming to laws of motion and scale. Modern scientific theories of how they follow a path is our interest.

The issue is that any concept we hold— like a season of the year— is considered something independently existing from the Periodical Table.  If those two were bridgable then the elements that composed them would be all-encompassing and deep.   A season is considered particular to our own human understanding in the scientific view.  We believe, because of science, that as humans brought into the world the notion of a season or it evolved somehow spontaneously during the course of cultural development.  By contrast, in the Five Elements a season of the year, an emotion is recognized as carrying  one or more of the Five Elements which in turn are composed of various proportions of Yin and Yang.  The properties of these Five Elements are seen as having been established when the universe was generated from Taiji (yin and yang) and the Wu Xing (Five Elements) exists because of Yin and Yang.  The existence of the seasons, emotions, the human body, its contents are interrelated because the world is essentially spiritual in nature, its order and arrangement is not accidental;  the living world is not an assortment of random accumulation because of physics and matter over long expanses time.  Rather, the long expanses of time have overseers.

The Periodic Table of Elements

The Five Elements (Wuxing) theory not only applies to natural phenomena and traditional practices but also aligns intriguingly with modern scientific concepts.

First, the atomic elements on the periodic chart are easily shown to be composed of the Five Elements:  phosphorus and sulfur, are made of the Fire element, known as combustible.  All of the metals on the table embody the Metal element materially as well.  Yet a metal like Magnesium that is combustible exhibits properties of both the Metal and Fire elements and is made of both.  Mercury is primarily composed of the Metal element but it exhibits properties of liquid, has a higher proportion of yin like the Water element and is thus made of both.  The application of intense cold (yin) or heat (yang) often changes the state of any of these elements so that they might be something else.  The elements in the Periodic Table are made out of the Five Elements, the Five Elements are made of yin and yang, so the addition of more yin or more yang would change one of its properties, which might be its physical state (solid, gas, liquid).
