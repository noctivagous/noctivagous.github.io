 Taiji (太極) and its core interactions 
 of Yin (陰) and Yang (陽).


Taiji as the Underlying Unity

Taiji represents the primordial unity or "Great Ultimate" from which 
all differentiation arises. It is the source of dynamic balance and 
perpetual change, symbolized by the swirling Taijitu (yin-yang diagram) 
where opposites are interdependent, they are mutually generating and 
mutually restraining, and in constant flux.  Nothing remains entirely static; 
each contain the presence of its opposite (the small dot of yang within 
yin, and vice versa).  There is yin within yang and yang within yin.

Key Taiji Principles

- mutual generation (相生), mutual restraint/control (相剋)

Yang can restrain yin.  Yin can restrain yang.  Yang can generate yin.
Yin can generate yang.

- interdependence
- reciprocal transformation (轉化)
- balance of ebb and flow


---


The 24 solar terms (節氣), where the Taiji curve is traditionally derived
is a smooth S-shaped progression reflecting gradual, inevitable transformation 
between extremes, with points of maximum/minimum (solstices) and balance 
(equinoxes).  The winter solstice is like night's midnight.  The summer solstice
is like daytime's noon.

Core Ancient Texts and Their Contributions

Zhouyi (周易) / Yijing (易經) – The Book of Changes 
(compiled ~9th–3rd centuries BCE, with commentaries 
like the Xici Zhuan / "Appended Phrases" from the Warring States period):
This is the foundational source for yin-yang interactions as the essence of 
change (易, yì). 
The famous line "一陰一陽之謂道" ("One yin and one yang is called the Dao") 
from the Xici Zhuan directly captures interdependence, 
reciprocal transformation, and ebb and flow (alternating waxing/waning).
It describes how yin and yang interact to produce the myriad things (萬物), 
with constant flux, mutual reliance (no pure yin or yang exists alone), 
and transformation through their union/separation.
The hexagrams and trigrams illustrate dynamic balance and cyclical change.

Huangdi Neijing (黃帝內經) – Yellow Emperor's Inner Classic 
(~2nd–1st centuries BCE, foundational TCM text):
Discusses yin-yang harmony, opposition, interdependence, and 
transformation extensively (e.g., in Suwen sections on cosmology and physiology).
Applies these to health: yin and yang must balance; excess or deficiency 
disrupts ebb/flow; mutual generation/restraint appears in organ interactions 
and seasonal cycles.  It integrates wuxing dynamics, where phases mutually 
generate and restrain to maintain harmony.

Chunqiu Fanlu (春秋繁露) – Luxuriant Dew of the Spring and Autumn Annals 
by Dong Zhongshu (~2nd century BCE):
Explicitly details wuxing xiangsheng (mutual generation) 
and xiangke / xiangsheng (mutual conquest/restraint) in dedicated chapters 
like "Wuxing Xiang Sheng" (Five Agents Mutual Generation) and related sections.
Describes cycles: e.g., wood generates fire, fire generates earth (generation); 
wood conquers earth, etc. (restraint/control).
Frames these as cosmic principles of balance, transformation, and 
interdependence in nature, politics, and human affairs.

Other Early Correlative Texts (Warring States to Han period):

Shujing (書經) / Book of Documents ("Hongfan" chapter / Great Plan): 
Early mention of wuxing as functional attributes (water soaks, fire blazes, etc.), laying groundwork for generation/restraint cycles.
Guanzi (管子) and Zuo Zhuan (左傳): Reference wuxing materials/elements and their interactions, including early ideas of mutual dependence and cyclical processes.
Huainanzi (淮南子) (~2nd century BCE): Synthesizes yin-yang and wuxing, emphasizing transformation, balance, and interdependence in cosmology.

How These Principles Are Framed

Mutual generation (相生) and mutual restraint (相剋): Primarily articulated 
in wuxing theory (generation cycle: wood → fire → earth → metal → water → wood; 
restraint cycle: wood → earth, etc.), as in Chunqiu Fanlu and later systematized 
in texts like Wuxing Dayi (五行大義) by Xiao Ji (Sui period, but drawing on Han 
sources). Interdependence and reciprocal transformation: Rooted in yin-yang 
duality (e.g., Yijing's "conjunction and alteration of yin and yang is called 
Dao"), where opposites rely on each other and convert (yang extreme births yin, 
and vice versa). Balance of ebb and flow: Evident in seasonal/solar cycles 
(24 solar terms), yin-yang alternation, and wuxing's cyclical nature—constant 
flux without permanent dominance.
