The Five Elements on a 2D Graph

Typically the Five Elements are introduced on a circular diagram showing how they affect one another, but for providing an introduction to such a foreign theory in our modern times this device is inadequate and confusing.  

Instead, here the Five Elements are presented first as a wave on a 2D graph.  This expresses their features in such a way there is more information about them, in particular their proportion of yin and yang at each point.  The y-axis plots Yang in the positive (up) direction and Yin in the negative (down) direction.  The x-axis will represent the progression of time if the elements are viewed in their relation to time.

[single sine wave]

The Five Elements are Water, Wood, Fire, Earth, and Metal and they contain various proportions of Yin and Yang.

A brief introduction to Yin and Yang gets the person started.  Yin is associated with the docile and what is contracting, heavy, dense, cold, and dark.  Yang is associated with the virile and what is expanding, buoyant, warm, and light.  Though these forces are opposites they are present at the same time and also complement each other. Their balance or proportion varies in any situation and they are in flux.  The seed of Yang is always present in Yin and vice-versa, as show in the Yin-Yang symbol.

Water is plotted at the trough of the wave.  Corresponding to this in the physical world is any natural trough, a low spot where water settles during cold seasons. The Water element is associated with what is Winter in many regions.  Even if precipitation is not highest during wintertime, water nevertheless lingers more in this period, either through snow or moisture persistence.  This time of year is cold, animals are dormant, and the daylight hours are at their shortest while the nighttime hours are at their longest; it is maximum Yin and minimum Yang. The energy is at its most inward and still, reflecting deep stillness and potential for renewal.  In the human life cycle, Winter (Water) represents old age, a period of life that has slowed down greatly, characterized by rest, reflection, and the accumulation of wisdom. It is also associated with the beginning (conception) of life, when dormancy and development is inward-focused, as in the case of the egg.

In terms of the time of day, Winter (Water) is associated with night, particularly midnight, when the world is at its darkest and most still. Emotionally, Winter (Water) is associated with fear (which always tries to be hidden), also reflecting the stillness and introspection of the season. In traditional Chinese medicine, Winter (Water) corresponds to the kidneys and bladder, organs associated with storing and purifying. Natural phenomena for Winter (Water) include snow, ice, and wildlife dormancy, reflecting the stillness and inward energy of the season. The colors associated with Winter (Water) are black and dark blue, colors that reflect the depth and stillness of the season. In terms of direction, Winter (Water) is associated with the north, reflecting cold and stillness. The flavor associated with Winter (Water) is salty, reflecting the preserving and purifying qualities of the season. In terms of developmental stages in projects or work, Winter (Water) represents conception and planning, a time of inward reflection and preparation.

When water freezes, it undergoes a process of cooling and becoming still, which are fundamentally Yin characteristics. The state of being a solid is also more Yin compared to the liquid state of water, as it is more stable and less active.  Additionally, the structure of ice, although solid and stable (Yin), also has a certain organized, crystalline structure that can be seen as embodying a form of order and structure (Yang).  The process of becoming ice involves the (Yang) active release of heat.

The above description describes the Five Elements in terms of progression of seasons.  The seasons lead into one another, each gradually transitions into the one that follows it which mirrors the Five Elements in how they transform and generate.  One element will lead to, produce, or generate the next in a cycle and the order of these seasons corresponds to that. This is the generative cycle (shēng) of the Five Elements.  The conventional four seasons are given the insertion of Late Summer before Autumn to make five.  The seasons make it easy to remember the order of the generating cycle.

Controlling Relationships, The Controlling (Kè) Cycle

The other process is the controlling (kè) one.  An element has a controlling effect over an element that is one steps past the one that it generates.  So, whereas the qualities of Winter (Water) produce or generates Spring (Wood), when talking about what Water controls it is Fire (Summer), which is one step past Spring. Water controls Fire; the set of qualities found in Winter control the qualities found in Summer.

In each instance there is a range of how much control the stronger element might exhibit over the subordinate one.  Water might be cooling off the heat and thereby controlling it, or it might be extinguishing the fire, destroying it.  Metal might be either pruning or felling a tree. As such, Ke is variously translated as controlling, insulting, or destroying.  It is acceptable to call kè the Controlling Cycle, Insulting Cycle, or Destroying Cycle.  An element might be doing one of these three to its receiver.

Understanding this kè process can take place by examining the relationship between the controlling element and the controlled as one of strength of firmness or ability to break up the receiving element.

The ke (controlling) cycle in the Wuxing (Five Elements) system can be understood in terms of an increasing gradient of firmness that repeats in which a firmer element generally controls, insults, or in the more intense case, destroys a less firm element.

We will discuss the cycle as a progression of firmness flows from the most firm element down. Metal (most firm) to Wood, Earth, Water, and finally to Fire (least firm). However, the cycle completes itself with a fascinating exception: Fire, the least firm element, controls Metal, the most firm. This unique relationship demonstrates the cyclical nature of the system and the transformative power of energy over structure. Each controlling relationship exhibits a range of interactions, from subtle influence to dramatic transformation.

Metal controlling Wood:

Metal, being firmer than Wood, can shape and limit it. A pruning shear can guide a plant's growth, demonstrating control. In the extreme, an axe can fell a tree, showing destruction. This range illustrates how precision can either direct or completely halt growth.

Metal's interaction with Wood is characterized by its sharpness and precision. This process’ relationship is often quick and definitive: chopping or slicing. Metal tools like axes, saws, or knives can easily cut through wood, shaping it decisively. In nature, this might be seen in how metal-rich soils can stunt plant growth. In human contexts, this could represent how clear, incisive thinking (Metal) can cut through and organize complex ideas or plans (Wood).

Wood controlling Earth:

Wood, firmer than Earth, can penetrate and restructure it. Plant roots can aerate soil, improving its quality, as a form of control. In the extreme, roots can break apart rocks and concrete over time, demonstrating destruction. This shows how growth, associated with Wood, can take place persistently and over time, which can either enhance or completely alter stable structures.

Wood's effect on Earth is typically a gradual process. Tree roots slowly grow through soil and rock, breaking them apart over time. This is a persistent, patient form of control. In less extreme cases, roots can improve soil structure by creating channels for air and water. This relationship might symbolize how steady personal growth can gradually change one's foundation or how innovative ideas can slowly transform established systems.

Earth controlling Water:

Earth, as a more solid element than Water, can contain and direct liquid Water. Riverbanks shape a river's flow, exemplifying control. In the extreme, a dam can completely stop a river, illustrating destruction. This range shows how stability can either guide or totally impede fluid movement.  There is constant surrounding or underlying of Water by Earth.

Water controlling Fire:

Water, while fluid, can temper and limit Fire. Steam can regulate heat in a system, demonstrating control. In the extreme, water can completely extinguish a fire, showing destruction. This illustrates how adaptability can either moderate or eliminate intense energy. There can be blanketing or dampening of Fire by Water.

Fire controlling Metal:

Fire, though least the firm of the Five Elements in terms of form, is the most active and can shape and transform rigid Metal. A controlled flame can temper metal, increasing its strength, as a form of control. In the extreme, intense heat can melt metal completely, demonstrating destruction. This range shows how transformative energy can either enhance or completely break down structure." Liquifying or increasing the malleability of Metal by Fire.

Metal has so much yin that it is rigid but not so much yin that it becomes inert.

The Five Elements Discussed in Terms of Yin and Yang

Each element is characterized by a unique balance of yin and yang forces, which gives rise to its distinctive properties and behaviors. Yin represents qualities such as coolness, contraction, and inward movement, while yang embodies heat, expansion, and outward motion.  Both are present in any situation and each contains the other.  The interplay between these complementary forces within each element creates a dynamic system that explains various phenomena in nature, health, and human affairs. Here we discuss how the proportions of yin and yang manifest in the properties of each of the Five Elements:

  1. Fire: Maximum yang, minimum yin. Fire has the highest yang and lowest yin, resulting in its gaseous, active, and energetic nature. This manifests in several key properties:
  • Expansive energy: High yang leads to rapid expansion and spread
  • Heat: Maximum yang manifests as intense heat and light
  • Upward movement: Strong yang energy causes rising motion
  • Transformation: High yang enables rapid change and conversion of matter
  • Low density: Minimum yin results in a lack of substance, leading to gaseous form

Lack of yin means lack of structure, form, and stability to fire.  It likewise does not adapt to anything in front of it or follow a provided path but goes its own way and expands, actively.  By contrast, maximum yin is characterized by stillness.

  1. Earth: Balanced yin and yang. Earth represents stability and balance, with roughly equal proportions of yin and yang. This balance manifests in the following ways:
  • Stability: Equal yin and yang create a grounded, stable nature
  • Nourishment: Balance allows for nurturing qualities
  • Centrality: Acts as a neutral point between other elements
  • Receptivity: Yin aspect allows absorption and containment
  • Production: Yang aspect enables growth and generation
  1. Metal: High yin, low yang. Metal's high yin and low yang composition explains its firm nature through several mechanisms:
  • Condensation: The high yin energy represents a condensing, contracting force, leading to increased density and firmness
  • Structure: Yin energy is associated with form and structure, contributing to its rigid, crystalline structure
  • Coolness: Yin is related to coolness, manifesting as resistance to deformation and a stable atomic structure
  • Inward-directed energy: Low yang means less outward, expansive energy, resulting in atoms bonding tightly, creating a solid, firm substance

Yin energy contributes to form and structure, making Metal rigid but not so much yin is normally present that Metal becomes a liquid like Water.  Mercury would be said to have a higher proportion of yin to make it normally a liquid.

Transforming Metal to Liquid: To transform Metal from its solid state to a liquid state, an infusion of Yang energy is necessary. This is where the Fire element comes into play.

  1. Water: Maximum yin, minimum yang. Water has the highest yin and lowest yang, which manifests as fluidity, adaptability, and downward movement. Water will flow and fill any shape or container, representing the ultimate form of receptivity and flexibility. This is why liquid water is seen as the embodiment of maximum Yin.  Its properties include:
  • Fluidity: High yin energy allows for adaptable, flowing nature
  • Coolness: Maximum yin manifests as a cooling, calming influence
  • Downward movement: Strong yin energy causes sinking or descending motion
  • Shapelessness: High yin leads to lack of fixed form, taking shape of its container
  • Depth: Strong yin creates potential for depth and accumulation

Even though maximum Yin produces water in the elements, Yin energy as a whole is crucial for the formation and maintenance of structures. While water represents Yin's fluidity and adaptability, Yin also provides the stability necessary for form and structure, as seen in the Metal element.

When water freezes, it undergoes a process of cooling and becoming still, which are fundamentally Yin characteristics. The state of being a solid is also more Yin compared to the liquid state of water, as it is more stable and less active.

  1. Wood: Moderate yang, moderate yin. Wood has a balance leaning slightly towards yang, resulting in growth, flexibility, and upward expansion, but with more structure than Fire. This is evident in its characteristics:
  • Growth: Slightly higher yang promotes upward and outward growth
  • Flexibility: Balance of yin and yang allows for bendability without breaking
  • Vitality: Moderate yang provides life force and energy
  • Structure: Moderate yin gives form and substance to support growth
  • Cyclical nature: Balance enables seasonal changes and regeneration

Confusion About Yin and Yang

The conventional dichotomies we have taken in from our modern Western education cannot be directly applied to Taiji, as this is something altogether different from what we were taught, the primal forces of Yin and Yang. Apparent contradictions weave, and there is genuine depth to the theory.

In Taiji, Yin and Yang are not simply opposites; they are complementary forces that interact in all things in the universe. The concepts of hardness and resilience in materials like steel, which in Western thought might be at first perceived as purely Yang characteristics, are understood in Taiji as part of a more complex interplay between Yin and Yang.

A piece of steel is indeed hard and resilient—qualities that are typically associated with Yang due to their firmness and ability to withstand pressure. However, Taiji theory goes beyond this superficial understanding. Yang represents energy, activity, and the potential for change, not just material hardness. This means that while steel's hardness can be seen as a Yang trait, the creation of a solid material involves both Yin and Yang. The Yin aspect provides the structural form, while the Yang aspect contributes to the dynamic properties like sharpness and the ability to cut or pierce.

Fire, as the element with maximum Yang and minimal Yin, embodies this energy and activity to the fullest extent. It represents the ultimate kinetic energy, demonstrating how Yang is not just about hardness or rigidity but about active, transformative power. In contrast, with minimal Yang, substances like Water, which remain liquid, highlight Yin's adaptability and structural properties without the active forcefulness of Yang.

The interplay between Yin and Yang is crucial. The Yang aspect of Metal, for example, is seen in its sharpness and dynamic qualities—its ability to cut and change. However, Metal itself is predominantly Yin, reflecting qualities such as contraction, stability, and structure. This shows that while Yang gives Metal its active properties, it is the Yin aspect that provides the foundational structure.

When Yang is applied in greater proportion to Yin, the resulting state can be solid, as seen when a liquid becomes sharp and solidified. But in maximum Yang, like Fire, we see a state of kinetic energy and dynamic strength that lacks the stability of structure. Conversely, in maximum Yin, we see conditions that produce a liquid state—fluid and adaptable but without the hardness that Yang brings.

Thus, the balance and proportion of Yin and Yang determine the qualities of a material or element. A small minority of Yang within a predominantly Yin context can produce a solid, structured material like Metal. Meanwhile, predominant Yin with minimal Yang results in a liquid state like Water, emphasizing adaptability and fluidity.

The Taiji strength (Yang) is not merely as material resistance but as a form of energy and potential for change. Yin provides the structure and form, but without the active influence of Yang, it remains fluid. The interaction of these primal forces creates the diverse states of matter and energy we observe.

Modern Science Versus The Five Elements

In the West, “reality” is considered a philosophical topic separate from investigation and descriptions of the physical world.  In ancient China the Wu Xing (Five Elements) - Water, Wood, Fire, Earth, and Metal - were regarded as the fundamental aspects of what we call “reality” at the same time they explain the constituents and processes of all things.  One could say they are more deep as elementary components; they extend beyond discrete, particulate units of the material, mechanistic reality we perceive in our modern age— which is what our atomic elements provide us conceptually.  If anything exists outside of these atomic elements or their subcomponents, we tend to believe it is our own human concepts projected onto the outside world.  If the opposite is permitted, that abstract ideas like the seasons, feelings, tastes, sounds, phases of human life, and even colors are not mere byproducts of our own psychological experience of matter, the Five Elements can be grasped as real and present in everything.  The world then has spiritual substance in addition to material presence.

In our modern times, we believe our world reality to be essentially molecularly-composed, all of it following physical laws of motion and interactions, whatever the latest physical theory (be it classical mechanics or later quantum descriptions).  What we engage with culturally and personally in this fundamentally dispassionate world of motion, energy, and matter are the human concepts we have projected onto it.  In our deeply-ingrained conception, nothing exists more than the motion of particles, their reactions, and how they can be described in various situations and levels of scale— now that we have all matured as a species.  In other words, we believe we live, basically, in a mundane space of interacting molecules and whatever subatomic elements and patterns coincide with all of it, from sounds to light, with none of it openly demonstrating anything spiritual we can hold onto, and until it does we have acted with blind faith and irrational.  We have come to think of the matter out there as without spirit, merely conforming to laws of science at scale, over time.  Modern scientific theories of how physical matter exists at the microscopic levels and acts at the macroscopic level is our interest, as it can provide solutions and practical amenities in the form of technology.

The subsequent issue is that any concept we hold— like a season of the year— is considered something independently existing from the Periodic Table.  If those two were bridgeable, the whatever theory that came forward composed would be all-encompassing and deep.   That is in fact what Yin and Yang and the Five Elements are, something lost and difficult to grasp for modern minds.  A season of the year is considered particular to our own human understanding, according to the scientific view.  We believe, because of science, that humans brought into the world the notion of a season, or our associations we placed on it evolved somehow spontaneously during the course of cultural development.  

By contrast, in the Five Elements an element spans the whole universe because humans are included in it: an emotion is recognized as carrying  one or more of the Five Elements.  The Five Elements are in turn composed of various proportions of Yin and Yang.  Animals emote just like humans do, but so does the planet during its various seasons.  

The properties of these Five Elements are seen as having been established when the universe was generated from Taiji (yin and yang) and so the Wu Xing (Five Elements) exists because of Yin and Yang.  The existence of the emotions, the human body, its organs and contents are interrelated because the world is essentially spiritual in nature, it has order and arrangement is not accidental; being alive is a substantial and spiritual matter.  The living world is not an assortment of accumulated processes because of what modern physics says matter does over long expanses of time.  Rather, the long expanses of time have overseers.  They set the stage in which humans live and it is all deeply interconnected.  But the theory to reveal this cannot come from the moderns, who are detached from wisdom, prize material experiences and proof at the expense of their spiritual welfare shortly before they reach the end of human life.

The Five Elements are Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water.  Wood comes from plants, mostly trees, so how can this end-product of plants, something as specific as the internal of a tree, be an element of all things in the world?  It must be symbolic somehow.  That isn’t the right understanding.  The Five Elements are regarded as symbolic by those who have not shifted their modern concepts.

The Five Elements are not for describing chemical constituents of objects alone. The Five Elements (Wuxing) in Chinese philosophy are ubiquitous and generic in applicability. They represent fundamental qualities present in any situation, paired with dynamic processes that can be found happening between them.  They can be applied not only to material and chemical situations (e.g. the Periodic Table, in which elements like Sulfur and Phosphorus are classified as Fire) but also to various aspects of life, nature, organs of the human body (the heart carrying the Fire element, the lungs made of the Metal element), and even what we consider abstract concepts of the mind, such as the seasons of a certain geographic region or process of starting up a business.  In the northern hemisphere, the season Winter is paired with Water as it is wet but in another region it would be summer paired with Water.  Winter might not have the most quantity precipitation (a modern scientific type of objection) but it is when water dwells and remains dormant along with the living world.

It is all situations that arise in life that can be described or conceived of that the Five Elements comprise.  Thus they are actually present elements during an undertaking because an undertaking is known as substantially real.  When all matter is understood as having thinking properties, this is a viewpoint that is more easily conceived.  It is a view that the universe is made of elements that encompass anything human beings know, observe, and experience, which means it is holistic.

The Periodic Table of Elements

The Five Elements (Wuxing) theory not only applies to natural phenomena and traditional practices but also aligns intriguingly with modern scientific concepts.

First, the atomic elements on the periodic chart are easily shown to be composed of the Five Elements:  phosphorus and sulfur, are made of the Fire element, known as combustible.  All of the metals on the table embody the Metal element materially as well.  Yet a metal like Magnesium that is combustible exhibits properties of both the Metal and Fire elements and is made of both.  Mercury is primarily composed of the Metal element but it exhibits properties of liquid, has a higher proportion of yin like the Water element and is thus made of both.  The application of intense cold (yin) or heat (yang) often changes the state of any of these elements so that they might be something else.  The elements in the Periodic Table are made out of the Five Elements, the Five Elements are made of yin and yang, so the addition of more yin or more yang would change one of its properties, which might be its physical state (solid, gas, liquid).

Yin is associated with both fluidity (as in water) and structure (as in metal). This apparent contradiction can be resolved by understanding yin energy's fundamental nature and how it manifests differently depending on its concentration and interaction with yang:

  1. Yin as condensation and containment: Yin energy fundamentally represents condensation, inward movement, and containment.   2. Yin as structure in different concentrations:
  • In water (maximum yin), the yin energy is so dominant that it creates a fluid structure. The molecules are close together (condensed) but not rigidly fixed, allowing for flowability.
  • In metal (high yin but not maximum), the yin energy creates a more rigid structure. There's enough yang present to provide some resistance to the total fluidity seen in water.
  1. The role of yang: The presence of some yang energy is what allows for solid structures. In water, the minimal yang means there's very little resistance to change of shape. In metal, the higher proportion of yang provides the energy for a more rigid bonding between atoms.
  2. Yin as potential and depth: Yin also represents potential and depth. In water, this manifests as its capacity to dissolve other substances and its ability to take on various forms (solid, liquid, gas) depending on conditions.

In essence, yin energy provides the tendency towards structure and form, but the exact nature of that structure depends on its concentration and interaction with yang. Maximum yin, as in water, creates a fluid structure that readily adapts to its surroundings, while high (but not maximum) yin, as in metal, creates a more rigid structure when balanced with a small amount of yang.

Yin and Yang Found in Meteorological Theories

The meteorological theory of low pressure and high pressure systems manifests core principles of yin and yang: opposing forces that are complementary, interconnected, and interdependent, constantly transforming into each other to maintain balance in nature.

  1. High Pressure Systems (Yang):
  • Active and expansive nature
  • Descending air creates clear skies and warmer temperatures
  • Pushes outward, influencing surrounding areas
  • Associated with stability and fair weather
  • Clockwise rotation in the Northern Hemisphere (counter-clockwise in Southern)
  • Represents the "giving" or "expressive" aspect
  1. Low Pressure Systems (Yin):
  • Receptive and contracting nature
  • Rising air creates clouds, precipitation, and cooler temperatures
  • Draws inward, pulling in surrounding air
  • Associated with change and unsettled weather
  • Counter-clockwise rotation in the Northern Hemisphere (clockwise in Southern)
  • Represents the "receiving" or "absorbing" aspect

The Microscale Describing the Macroscale

A hot (yang) shower will release steam upwards and outwards in the bathroom, but the addition of cold air temperature (yin) by turning on air conditioning will make the steam contract (yin) and fall (yin).  The condensation of the water is a yin process, making it denser and heavier, falling downward (yin). The initial expansion and dispersal (yang) of water through heat (yang) made it less dense and therefore buoyant in the room, making it rise (yang).

Temperature affects the pressure in any weather system, but the principles discussed for large-scale weather systems are also occurring on a smaller scale in the shower room. Let's examine this microcosm:

  1. In the shower room:
  • The hot shower creates warm, moist air that expands and rises (yang).
  • This rising air creates a localized area of lower pressure near the ceiling.
  • The cooler air from the air conditioning contracts and sinks (yin).
  • This sinking air creates areas of higher pressure near the floor.
  1. Circulation pattern:
  • The warm air's rise and the cool air's descent create a small-scale circulation within the bathroom.
  • This mimics larger atmospheric circulation patterns, just on a much smaller scale.
  1. Pressure differences:
  • While the pressure differences in the bathroom are much smaller than in weather systems, they still exist and drive air movement.
  1. Condensation and precipitation:
  • As in larger weather systems, the rising warm, moist air cools as it meets the cooler air or surfaces.
  • This leads to condensation, forming water droplets on surfaces or falling as tiny "rain" droplets in the room.

The shower room acts as a miniature model of atmospheric processes. The principles governing large-scale weather systems are indeed at play in this small enclosed space, demonstrating the scalability of these physical laws.

This in itself is a topic in ancient science which is that the macrocosm reflects the microcosm.  

This comparison highlights how fundamental physical principles operate across different scales, from the tiny confines of a shower room to the vast expanses of global weather systems. It's a great example of how observing everyday phenomena can lead to understanding larger natural processes.

The interaction between high and low pressure systems creates the dynamic weather patterns we experience, much like the interplay of yin and yang in ancient Chinese science:

  • Balance: Neither high nor low pressure systems dominate permanently; they exist in a constant state of flux.
  • Interdependence: High pressure systems often lead to the formation of low pressure systems in adjacent areas, and vice versa.
  • Transformation: As conditions change, high pressure can weaken and become low pressure, and low can strengthen into high.
